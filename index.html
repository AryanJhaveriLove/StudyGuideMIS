Chapter 6 Study Guide

Moore's Law and More: Fast/Cheap Computing and What This Means for the Manager

This study guide includes precise vocabulary, clear explanations, and a 5-question multiple choice quiz for review.

What to Know

This chapter is about how fast computing improves, why it gets cheaper over time, and how managers should think about the business impact of those changes.

Main Theme

Technology improves exponentially, not linearly. When computing becomes cheaper, companies discover new uses for it and entire industries can change.

Manager Takeaway

Managers need to ask: What becomes possible when computing is dramatically faster and cheaper?

Vocabulary

Moore's Law

Definition: Moore's Law states that the number of transistors on a microchip doubles approximately every 18 to 24 months, leading to exponential growth in computing power.

Why it matters: It helps explain why technology becomes faster and cheaper over time.

Example: A smartphone today is more powerful than many older high-end computers.

Exponential Growth

Definition: Exponential growth is growth that increases at an accelerating rate rather than by the same amount each period.

Why it matters: Managers often underestimate how quickly technological capability can improve.

Example: AI performance improving very rapidly over a short period of time.

Price Elasticity of Demand

Definition: Price elasticity of demand measures how strongly demand changes when price changes. In technology markets, demand is often highly elastic.

Why it matters: When computing gets cheaper, people want more of it and discover new uses for it.

Example: As cloud storage prices fell, businesses and consumers stored far more data online.

Falling Cost of Computing

Definition: The falling cost of computing refers to the continued decline in the cost of processing power, storage, and network capacity.

Why it matters: Lower costs make new business models and products possible.

Example: Streaming platforms became practical because storing and delivering large media files became more affordable.

Disruptive Innovation

Definition: Disruptive innovation occurs when a new technology or business model begins in a weaker or simpler form and eventually changes or replaces established markets.

Why it matters: Cheap computing often enables disruptive innovations that challenge established firms.

Example: Generative AI is changing how customer service, marketing, and content creation are performed.

Digital Convergence

Definition: Digital convergence is the merging of multiple technologies or devices into one product or platform.

Why it matters: It reduces the need for separate devices and creates more value for users.

Example: Smartphones combine a camera, GPS, music player, web browser, and phone into one device.

Computationally Intensive Applications

Definition: Computationally intensive applications are programs that require large amounts of processing power to function.

Why it matters: These applications become more practical as computing power increases and costs fall.

Example: Machine learning models and generative AI systems require substantial computing resources.

Managerial Blind Spot

Definition: A managerial blind spot occurs when a manager fails to recognize the speed or importance of technological change.

Why it matters: This can cause firms to miss opportunities or respond too slowly to disruption.

Example: A firm ignoring artificial intelligence until competitors have already adopted it at scale.

Key Concepts

  • Computing power tends to improve exponentially rather than linearly.
  • As the price of computing falls, businesses and consumers find more ways to use it.
  • Cheaper computing can enable new industries, products, and business models.
  • Managers must think ahead instead of assuming technology will only improve slowly.
  • Fast, cheap computing can create major competitive threats and opportunities.

Interactive Practice Quiz

1. A student startup is building an AI note-taking app for UT students. Two years ago, the team could not afford enough computing power to run speech-to-text and summary tools at a useful level. Now, cloud computing prices have dropped, and the app can offer those features at a student-friendly price. The founders are debating whether this shift is mostly about better strategy, stronger branding, or a basic change in technology economics.

Which concept BEST explains why the startup can now offer advanced features to many more students?

2. A campus job board used to be slow and hard to search, so students ignored it. Over the last few years, faster hardware and cheaper storage let the university add filters, instant search, resume matching, and AI suggestions without charging students. One manager says the upgrade happened gradually, but another says the real lesson is that small technical improvements can add up and create much bigger business changes than people expect.

What is the MOST accurate reason managers often misjudge changes like this?

3. A student compares her phone to the devices her parents carried in college: a phone, camera, GPS, iPod, flashlight, and paper planner. She realizes one modern device now performs all of those roles well enough for daily use. A classmate argues that this is just an example of brand loyalty, but her professor says the better explanation is that cheaper and more powerful computing let many digital functions merge into one product.

Which concept BEST fits this scenario?

4. A small tutoring company avoided using AI tools last year because the software was too expensive and too slow for everyday work. This year, the same company uses AI to draft practice questions, summarize readings, and organize lesson plans because those tasks now run quickly and cheaply enough to fit a normal budget. The owner says the company did not suddenly become more creative; instead, the economics of computing changed what was practical.

Which interpretation is MOST consistent with Moore’s Law and this chapter’s discussion?

5. During a strategy meeting, an intern says that because laptops are already fast, future hardware improvements will not matter very much to business decisions. Another intern argues that even modest improvements can unlock new products when those gains happen repeatedly over time and at lower cost. The manager wants to know which view better matches how computing usually affects markets.

Which response is BEST?
