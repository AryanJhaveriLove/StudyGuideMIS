Chapter 8 Study Guide

Data, Information, and Business Intelligence

This chapter explains how firms collect, store, and analyze data to make better decisions, and how raw data becomes useful information and insight.

What to Know

This chapter is about how data is stored, processed, and turned into insights that managers can use.

Main Theme

Raw data has little value on its own. Value comes from turning data into information and insight through analysis.

Manager Takeaway

Managers should focus on asking the right questions and using data to support decisions, not just collecting more data.

Vocabulary

Data

Definition: Data are raw facts or observations that have not yet been processed or interpreted.

Why it matters: Data alone does not create value until it is organized and analyzed.

Example: A list of sales transactions is data.

Information

Definition: Information is processed data that has meaning and context.

Why it matters: Information helps managers understand what is happening in the business.

Example: Total weekly sales by region is information.

Business Intelligence (BI)

Definition: Business intelligence refers to technologies and practices used to analyze data and support decision-making.

Why it matters: BI tools help managers turn large amounts of data into useful insights.

Example: Dashboards and reports that show trends and performance metrics.

Database

Definition: A database is an organized collection of data stored electronically.

Why it matters: Databases allow firms to efficiently store and retrieve data.

Example: A customer database storing names, purchases, and contact details.

Data Warehouse

Definition: A data warehouse is a large centralized repository that stores data from multiple sources for analysis.

Why it matters: It allows firms to analyze data across different departments and time periods.

Example: Combining sales, marketing, and finance data into one system.

Data Mining

Definition: Data mining is the process of analyzing large datasets to find patterns, trends, or relationships.

Why it matters: It helps firms discover insights that are not obvious.

Example: Finding that customers who buy one product often buy another.

Big Data

Definition: Big data refers to extremely large datasets that are difficult to process using traditional tools.

Why it matters: Big data allows for deeper analysis but requires advanced tools and systems.

Example: Social media activity, sensor data, and large-scale transaction records.

Structured Data

Definition: Structured data is organized in a predefined format, such as rows and columns.

Why it matters: It is easier to store and analyze using traditional databases.

Example: Data in spreadsheets or relational databases.

Unstructured Data

Definition: Unstructured data does not have a fixed format and is harder to analyze.

Why it matters: Much of today’s data is unstructured and requires advanced tools to process.

Example: Emails, videos, images, and social media posts.

Data Visualization

Definition: Data visualization is the presentation of data in graphical form.

Why it matters: Visuals make it easier to understand patterns and trends.

Example: Charts, graphs, and dashboards.

Dashboards

Definition: Dashboards are visual displays of key metrics and data.

Why it matters: They provide quick insights for decision-making.

Example: A dashboard showing sales, profit, and customer metrics.

Predictive Analytics

Definition: Predictive analytics uses data and models to forecast future outcomes.

Why it matters: It helps firms anticipate trends and make proactive decisions.

Example: Predicting future sales based on past data.

Key Concepts

  • Data must be processed into information to be useful.
  • Business intelligence tools help analyze and visualize data.
  • Data warehouses combine data from multiple sources for analysis.
  • Big data creates opportunities but requires advanced tools.
  • Visualization and dashboards make data easier to understand.
