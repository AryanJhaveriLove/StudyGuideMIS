Chapter 1 Study Guide

Setting the Stage: Technology and the Modern Enterprise

This chapter introduces the foundations of information systems, why communication is difficult, and how mental models influence both technology design and business decisions.

What to Know

This chapter is about understanding information systems as more than just technology. It also explains how people think, communicate, and miscommunicate when using systems.

Main Theme

Technology works inside a broader system of data, processes, and human interpretation. Bad assumptions and weak communication can undermine even good tools.

Manager Takeaway

Managers need to understand both the technical and human sides of information systems so they can communicate clearly and make better decisions.

Vocabulary

Information System (IS)

Definition: An information system is a combination of hardware, software, data, and processes that works together to collect, process, store, and communicate information.

Why it matters: Managers are not just dealing with technology by itself. They are dealing with the entire system that produces useful information for decisions and operations.

Example: A university course registration platform depends on computers, software, stored student data, and the process used to add and drop classes.

Mental Model

Definition: A mental model is a learned way of thinking about how the world works.

Why it matters: People design systems and interpret information through mental models, so different assumptions can produce different system designs and decisions.

Example: Two managers may read the same dashboard differently because they have different assumptions about what the numbers mean.

Curse of Knowledge

Definition: The curse of knowledge is the tendency for people who already know something to have difficulty imagining what it is like not to know it.

Why it matters: It makes experts poor communicators when they assume that others already understand their background knowledge.

Example: A professor explaining Excel formulas in technical language may think the explanation is clear even when students are confused.

Hardware

Definition: Hardware is the physical equipment used in computing, such as computers, servers, phones, and networking devices.

Why it matters: Hardware is one of the core components of an information system and affects what software and processes can run.

Example: Laptops in a classroom and servers in a data center are hardware.

Software

Definition: Software is the set of instructions that tells hardware what to do.

Why it matters: Software determines how users interact with data and how the system performs tasks.

Example: Excel, Canvas, and web browsers are software.

Data

Definition: Data are raw facts, figures, or observations that can be stored and processed by an information system.

Why it matters: Information systems are only as useful as the quality and relevance of the data they contain and communicate.

Example: Student IDs, prices, and inventory counts are data.

Process

Definition: A process is a set of steps or activities used to complete a task or achieve an outcome.

Why it matters: Software usually reflects assumptions about how a process should work, so weak process design can create weak systems.

Example: An order fulfillment process may include taking an order, checking stock, shipping the item, and recording the sale.

Technology Buzzwords

Definition: Technology buzzwords are popular terms used to sound informed about technology without necessarily understanding the actual concepts behind them.

Why it matters: The chapter stresses that real technology literacy comes from understanding concepts, not just repeating trendy language.

Example: Using terms like “AI” or “cloud” without knowing what they mean is relying on buzzwords instead of knowledge.

Technology Literacy

Definition: Technology literacy is the ability to understand and use core technology concepts in a thoughtful and practical way.

Why it matters: Managers need enough literacy to evaluate technology, communicate with others, and avoid being misled by hype.

Example: A manager who understands the basic parts of an information system can ask better questions before adopting a new tool.

Communication in Information Systems

Definition: Communication in information systems refers to how information is transmitted, interpreted, and understood across the system.

Why it matters: Since information systems exist to communicate information, misunderstandings in communication can create poor decisions and weak system outcomes.

Example: A report may contain accurate data, but if managers interpret it differently, the information system still fails to support good action.

Key Concepts

  • Information systems are broader than just software or hardware.
  • Mental models influence how people design systems and interpret information.
  • The curse of knowledge makes communication especially difficult between experts and nonexperts.
  • Technology literacy requires understanding concepts instead of relying on buzzwords.
  • Even a strong technical tool can fail if the surrounding data and processes are weak.

Interactive Practice Quiz

1. A company buys new inventory software, but employees are still entering data inconsistently, managers disagree about the approval steps, and different departments interpret the reports differently. One executive says the firm’s information system problem should disappear because the software itself has already been upgraded.

Which response BEST reflects Chapter 1?

2. A professor explains a spreadsheet formula using technical language that makes perfect sense to her, but her students remain confused. She keeps repeating the same explanation and assumes the students simply are not paying attention.

3. Two managers review the same dashboard and reach different conclusions about what the numbers mean. One sees the figures as proof that demand is growing, while the other believes the same figures show operations are becoming inefficient. Neither manager is lying; they are simply interpreting the information differently.

What is the BEST explanation for this difference?

4. A student is told to learn a new software tool on her own by comparing help pages, tutorials, and examples. She notices that some online advice conflicts, especially when people use different operating systems or are giving opinions instead of facts.

Which lesson from Chapter 1 is MOST relevant?

5. A manager says, “Technology strategy is easy. I just need to know the latest buzzwords so I sound current in meetings.” Another manager disagrees and says that long-term success requires understanding the underlying concepts instead of repeating fashionable terms.

Which view is MOST consistent with the chapter?
